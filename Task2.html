What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cybercrime where attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to steal sensitive information such as usernames, passwords, credit card details, and other personal data.

Phishing attacks can lead to identity theft, financial loss, corporate data breaches, and damage to your organization's reputation. Everyone is a potential target!

How Phishing Works

  1. Bait: Attackers send fraudulent emails, texts, or create fake websites
  2. Hook: They create urgency or fear to prompt immediate action
  3. Capture: Victims provide sensitive information or download malware
  4. Exploit: Attackers use stolen data for fraud or further attacks

Types of Phishing Attacks

1. Email Phishing

The most common form where attackers send mass emails impersonating trusted organizations.

Common Targets: Banks, social media platforms, online retailers, government agencies

2. Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks focused on specific individuals or organizations using personal information.

Example: An email appearing to be from your CEO asking for urgent wire transfer

3. Whaling

Spear phishing attacks specifically targeting high-profile executives or important individuals.

4. Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing attacks conducted through text messages.

Example: "Your bank account has been compromised. Click here to verify: bit.ly/fake-link"

5. Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phone-based attacks where scammers impersonate legitimate organizations.

6. Clone Phishing

Attackers create nearly identical copies of legitimate emails with malicious links or attachments.

7. Pharming

Redirecting users from legitimate websites to fraudulent ones without their knowledge.

Real-World Examples

Suspicious Email Example #1

From: security-alert@amazom.com RED FLAG
Subject: URGENT: Your account will be suspended! RED FLAG

Dear Customer,

We have detected suspicious activity on your account. Your account will be suspended within 24 hours unless you verify your information immediately.

Click here to verify now: http://amazom-security.net/verify RED FLAG

Thank you,
Amazon Security Team

Red Flags Identified:
  • Misspelled domain (amazom.com instead of amazon.com)
  • Creates false urgency
  • Suspicious URL that doesn't match Amazon's domain
  • Generic greeting ("Dear Customer")

Legitimate Email Example

Legitimate Indicators:
  • Correct domain name
  • Specific personal information (name, order number)
  • No urgent action required
  • Directs to main website, not suspicious links

How to Detect Phishing

üîç Email Red Flags

  • Sender's email address doesn't match the organization
  • Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" or "Dear User"
  • Urgent language creating false sense of emergency
  • Spelling and grammar mistakes
  • Requests for sensitive information via email
  • Suspicious links (hover to see real destination)
  • Unexpected attachments, especially executables
  • Mismatched logos or branding inconsistencies

üîó Link Analysis

Always verify links before clicking:

Hover Test: Hover your mouse over links to see the actual destination URL in the bottom-left corner of your browser.

Suspicious URL patterns:

  • Shortened URLs (bit.ly, tinyurl.com) from unknown sources
  • Misspelled domains (gooogle.com, amazom.com)
  • Random subdomains (amazon.fake-site.com)
  • Unusual top-level domains (.tk, .ml for major companies)

üì± Mobile-Specific Warning Signs

  • SMS from unknown numbers asking for personal info
  • Apps requesting excessive permissions
  • Pop-ups claiming your device is infected
  • Fake app store notifications

‚òéÔ∏è Phone Call Red Flags

  • Callers asking for passwords or PINs
  • High-pressure tactics creating urgency
  • Requests to install remote access software
  • Unsolicited calls about computer problems

    üõ°Ô∏è Essential Security Practices

    • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts
    • Use strong, unique passwords for each account
    • Keep software and security systems updated
    • Install reputable antivirus software
    • Regularly backup important data
    • Be cautious with public Wi-Fi networks
    • Verify unexpected communications through official channels
    • Think before you click - when in doubt, don't

    üö® If You Think You've Been Phished

    Immediate Actions:
    1. Don't panic - Quick action can minimize damage
    2. Disconnect - Disconnect from the internet if possible
    3. Change passwords - Update compromised accounts immediately
    4. Contact your bank - If financial info was compromised
    5. Report the incident - Notify IT security and relevant authorities
    6. Monitor accounts - Watch for unauthorized activity
    7. Run security scans - Check for malware
